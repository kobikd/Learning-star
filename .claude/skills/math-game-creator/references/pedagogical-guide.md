## Core Philosophy {#core-philosophy}

The goal is to **remove barriers** so the child can think mathematically — not to turn math
into yet another arena of motor/sensory/executive struggle.

Five organizing principles:
1. Build a base of **clarity and predictability** (fixed structure, fixed language, clean pages)
2. Teach **explicitly and systematically** (explicit + systematic)
3. Provide **graded representations** with visual anchors (concrete → semi-concrete → abstract)
4. **Reduce working memory and executive function load**
5. **Monitor progress** frequently with small measures

Working assumptions:
- The child speaks Hebrew as her primary language
- She is in a regular or inclusive classroom with supports
- "High functioning" means principled academic learning ability, but with challenges in
  flexibility, planning, regulation, and execution
- The mathematical challenge may involve not just "number understanding" but also
  planning of movement/timing/regulation
- There is significant variability in autism math profiles — this guide is built for
  individual adaptation

## Developmental Profile at Age 8 {#developmental-profile}

At age 8, children are typically in the **concrete operational thinking** stage:
- They can understand relationships, patterns, and rules when they are "visible" —
  supported by materials, examples, and consistency
- There is a leap in persistence, understanding social rules, and independence
- BUT there is still high dependence on adult mediation when task load is high

In the Israeli curriculum (grades 1-3), the central focus is "numbers and operations"
alongside "geometry and measurement," with a shift from emphasizing computational
speed/accuracy alone toward numerical understanding, mathematical discourse, and
explaining solution strategies.

## How DCD Affects Math {#dcd-and-math}

DCD (Developmental Coordination Disorder / dyspraxia) is defined as a neurodevelopmental
disorder manifested in difficulties executing fine and gross motor skills that impact
daily and academic functioning.

Impact on math specifically:
- **Writing load**: Slow, effortful writing; illegible handwriting; disorganized pages;
  avoidance of written tasks
- **Place value/digit alignment**: Errors like writing 307 instead of 370 due to unstable
  visual cues. Research shows visuoperceptual skills are linked to aspects of mathematical
  difficulty in DCD.
- **Executive functions**: Relative weakness in inhibition, working memory, and planning
  can make it hard to maintain steps, choose strategies, and monitor errors

**Critical point**: If we "demand" that the child demonstrate math knowledge through a
dense, fast writing channel, we may end up measuring the motor difficulty (or executive
load) rather than the understanding.

## ASD Characteristics Relevant to Math {#asd-and-math}

Three axes worth planning around:
1. **Sensory regulation**: DSM-5 includes hyper/hypo-reactivity to sensory stimuli.
   Sensory profile affects classroom participation and learning.
2. **Executive functions**: Meta-analyses point to characteristic (though not uniform)
   difficulties in inhibition, flexibility, planning, and working memory — especially
   when tasks require transitioning between rules or multi-step organization.
3. **Social communication & language comprehension**: Word problems require decoding
   intent, understanding relationships, and sometimes social language ("gives", "remains",
   "more than"). Children strong in calculation can still have specific difficulty with
   word problems without visual/structural mediation.

### Cognitive Profiles to Consider

Research indicates autistic individuals may have lower average math ability compared to
same-age peers, but with much higher variability — some children are very strong in certain
areas and weak in others.

In corrective instruction, it makes sense to address 4 cognitive "direction buttons":
- **Working memory**: Does the child "lose" steps?
- **Processing speed**: Does she know but slowly? (affects practice, tests, load)
- **Visual-spatial**: Is there confusion in direction/alignment/similar numbers?
- **Language & instruction comprehension**: Is the problem "who asked what" and not the calculation?

## Mild Intellectual Disability — Adaptations {#mid-adaptations}

The child also has mild intellectual disability, which adds specific requirements beyond DCD+ASD:

### Cognitive Adaptations
- **Minimal instructions**: One sentence + one picture per instruction. Never more.
- **Heavy repetition**: Every concept must be repeated 5-7+ times in different visual contexts
  before she can internalize it. Don't assume she "got it" after 2-3 successes.
- **Built-in success**: 70-80% of tasks should be at already-mastered level. Only 20-30%
  should be genuinely new or challenging.
- **Graduated scaffolding**: hint → stronger hint → full demonstration → guided action.
  Never jump from "no help" to "here's the answer."
- **Simple language**: Short words, short sentences, easy Hebrew. Always with full nikud.
- **Concrete stays longer**: She will need more time at the concrete and representational
  stages of CRA before moving to abstract. Don't rush to symbols.

### Errorless Learning for MID
The errorless learning approach is especially important here:
- **Start with 2 choices**: 1 correct, 1 obviously very different. This ensures near-certain
  success and builds confidence.
- **Gradually increase to 3 choices** as she masters the concept.
- **Never start with 4+ choices** — too overwhelming.
- The wrong option in 2-choice mode should be very obviously wrong (e.g., if the answer is 7,
  the wrong option is 20, not 8). As she improves, make distractors closer.

### Processing & Memory
- She may process more slowly — never rush, never time-pressure.
- Working memory may be more limited — keep all relevant info visible on screen.
- Abstract concepts take longer to form — always pair with visual representation.
- She may need the same type of problem many times before the pattern "clicks."

## Evidence-Based Strategies {#strategies}

| # | Strategy | What It Does | Why It Fits Here | Watch Out For |
|---|----------|-------------|-----------------|---------------|
| 1 | **Explicit & Systematic Instruction** | Breaks skills into steps; example, guided practice, independent practice | Reduces working memory load; supports executive function; allows consistent reinforcement | Too many steps/words on one page; too fast pace; too much reliance on signs |
| 2 | **Graded Representations** (Concrete→Semi-concrete→Abstract) | "See" via objects/diagrams before symbols | Supports concrete thinking at age 8; reduces errors from premature abstraction | Moving to abstract too early; premature symbol location |
| 3 | **CRA Framework** (Concrete-Representational-Abstract) | Structured framework for transitions between representations | Useful for students with learning gaps/disabilities; adaptable to materials | "Disconnect" between stages if not explaining connections |
| 4 | **Number Line as Central Strategy** | Comparison, addition/subtraction, building number sense | Reduces density; small visual anchor; consistent directional cue | Dense line; small symbols; direction not consistent |
| 5 | **Short, Fixed Math Vocabulary** | Vocabulary and sentence patterns | Eases word problems and communication/pragmatic load | Synonyms, redundant words, compound ("more", "add", "plus" on same page) |
| 6 | **Schema-Based Problem Solving** (Bar model) | Identifies "problem type" and uses fixed diagrams | Reduces load; increases consistency; raises success in word problems | Too many schemas too fast; complex diagrams; complex language |
| 7 | **Errorless Learning** (Prompting + Fading) | Ensures high success with gradually decreasing cues | Reduces anxiety/avoidance; fits acquisition skills; suitable for ASD action sequence | Staying with hints too long; no transition to independence |
| 8 | **Action-by-Action Reinforcement & Feedback** | Immediate, short, specific feedback on clear step | Builds motivation; stabilizes participation; supports regulation | Vague feedback ("nice") instead of specific; "direct grading" instead of specific |
| 9 | **UDL Design** (Universal Design for Learning) | Multiple options for representation, action, expression, engagement | Enables flexible materials (e.g., circling instead of writing) | "Systematic" embedding without measurement and reliability in implementation |

## Material Adaptations for DCD+ASD {#adaptations}

| Component | Recommended Adaptation | Why It Helps | Example |
|-----------|----------------------|-------------|---------|
| **Layout** | Lots of "air," large answer boxes, 1-2 columns | Reduces visual and motor load | Wide answer box instead of narrow line in each exercise |
| **Place value** | Grid/template for digit alignment | Reduces spatial errors and organization | Hundreds-tens-ones table next to each exercise |
| **Instructions** | One instruction line + fixed icon always with same symbol | Supports consistency and executive functions | "הקיפי" always with the same icon |
| **Representations** | Concrete/semi-concrete before abstract | Supports conceptual understanding | Base-10 cubes before 3-digit numbers |
| **Response options** | Choice between 3 answers instead of writing | Doesn't "punish" grapho-motor difficulty | Circling/matching/clicking instead of writing |
| **Pacing** | Segments of 5-10 min + defined visual timer breaks | Reduces sensory/fatigue exposure | Visual timer, defined stop, micro-break after 10 min |
| **Feedback** | Immediate, specific per step, 1 sentence | Builds confidence and direction toward goal | "התחלת מהמאות — זה עוזר!" |
| **Language mediation** | Small vocabulary card on page | Eases word problems | Card: "ביחד=+" "נשאר=−" |

## Do's and Don'ts {#dos-and-donts}

### Do (כן לעשות):
- One page = one goal (or maximum two close goals)
- Fixed structure repeating between pages: title → example → short practice → large answer space
- Small but quality number of items: 6-10 good items preferred over 30 dense exercises
- Digit alignment and place-value grid in almost every multi-digit calculation
- Short instructions with one verb ("circle", "click", "connect")
- High success at start of unit (80-90%) then graduated difficulty increase — especially if there is avoidance or anxiety
- Options to answer without writing: circling, matching, digital dragging, number cards
- Short, specific feedback on "what worked": "שמתי לב שהשתמשת בקו מספרים!"

### Don't (לא לעשות):
- Don't densify (Dense worksheets): density increases errors, fatigues, punishes motor difficulty
- Don't switch words for the same operation on the same page ("עוד/נוסף/קיבל") without teaching them explicitly
- Don't mix different problem types before there is basic "fluency" for each type
- Don't link math assessment to writing/copying speed — if practicing fluency, do it in a controlled, short, monitored way, with sensory accommodation, not as basis for grades
- Don't use color as a sole "meaning carrier" (e.g., just "the red is tens") — to not overload sensory/visual perception

## Hebrew-Specific Considerations {#hebrew-considerations}

- **Bidirectional reality**: In Hebrew, text generally flows right-to-left, but math symbols
  and numbers are sometimes drawn left-to-right. The Academy sets rules for writing
  numerical ranges in Hebrew, which emphasizes the importance of consistency and adherence
  to writing rules to reduce load.
- **Mental number line**: Research on number processing in Hebrew speakers points to the
  complexity of the relationship between writing direction and numerical representation.
  Therefore, in building materials it is worthwhile to pay attention to commas, alignment,
  and consistent number lines.
- **Number line direction**: Always left-to-right (small to large), with clear arrows.
  This matches the universal mathematical convention and reduces confusion.

## Sample Lesson Structures {#lesson-structures}

### Number Line Addition (20-25 min)
Goal: Child solves 6-8 simple addition/subtraction exercises using jumps on number line.
1. Warm-up (2 min): Identify "small/large" on a large printed number line
2. Demonstration (5 min): Teacher solves 2 examples aloud with finger on number line
3. Guided practice (8 min): 4 exercises — child points, teacher confirms
4. Independent practice (5 min): 2-3 exercises with circling (no writing)
5. Closure (2 min): Fixed summary sentence: "בחיבור אני זזה קדימה, בחיסור אחורה"

### Place Value 3-Digit Numbers (25 min)
Goal: Child matches base-10 representation to number and doesn't swap between tens and ones.
Emphasis: First build with cubes/representations, then choice/circling — writing only if it's not an obstacle.

### Word Problem "Part-Part-Whole" (20 min)
Goal: Child identifies problem type and uses a fixed diagram.
Mediation: 3-step fixed card; small vocabulary dictionary; practice on the same "template" with changing numbers.

## Assessment & Progress Monitoring {#assessment}

Good assessment in this case divides between three things:
(a) What the child **understands** mathematically
(b) What she is **capable of performing** when the writing/coordination/regulation channel enters
(c) What happens in "real performance" at home/classroom

### Measuring Progress Without Overloading
- Measure "one step" each week: focus on one skill only (e.g., "addition on number line up to 20")
- Maintain fixed format of the check (same page type, same time, same response method) —
  otherwise you're measuring adaptation to change, not math
- Distinguish between "accuracy" and "independence": if the child succeeds with a hint —
  that's progress, but you need to plan gradual hint reduction (fading)

## Learning Star Project Integration {#learning-star}

The games created by this skill are designed to align with the כוכב הלמידה (Learning Star) app —
a React/TypeScript PWA. While the skill outputs standalone HTML files (for quick prototyping and
standalone use), the design patterns match the app's architecture:

### Color Palette (must match)
```css
--bg-primary: #F0F4FF;        /* light lavender */
--bg-secondary: #FFF8F0;      /* warm cream */
--color-math: #7C6FEB;        /* soft purple — math activities */
--color-success: #6BCB77;     /* soft green — correct answers */
--color-try-again: #FFA07A;   /* salmon — encouragement, NOT red */
--color-stars: #FFD700;        /* gold — stars */
--text-primary: #2D3748;      /* dark but not black */
```

### Typography (must match)
- Primary font: 'Assistant', fallback: 'Rubik', sans-serif
- Line-height: 2.0 (high for nikud comfort)
- Sizes: instructions 28px, content 24px, buttons 26px, numbers/letters 48px, titles 36px
- ALL text with full nikud

### Touch Targets (must match)
- Minimum 64×64px buttons (80px+ preferred)
- 16px+ padding around every interactive element
- Drag-and-drop with 150%+ hit area magnet snap
- Single tap only — no double-click, no long-press
- Movement tolerance on taps

### Sound System (when implemented)
- UI feedback: soft-pop (click), whoosh-gentle (transition), sparkle (open)
- Correct answer: happy-chime (short, positive, < 2 sec)
- Try again: soft-nudge (encouraging, NOT critical)
- Streak of 3: celebration-short
- NO failure/error sounds ever
- All sounds off by default, togglable
- Use the `/gentle-learning-sfx` skill for any sound design

### Reward System (must match)
- Stars: earned per correct answer. Bonus star for streak of 3.
- Stickers: earned per completed activity. Collection in sticker album.
- Stars NEVER decrease on mistakes.
- No rankings or comparisons to others.
- No time pressure.
- No negative messages of any kind.

### Math Curriculum Structure (Israeli 2nd Grade)
The games should map to these skill IDs when possible:
- Number sense: ns1 (1-10), ns2 (11-20), ns3 (up to 100)
- Addition: add1 (up to 10), add2 (up to 20, crossing 10), add3 (tens)
- Subtraction: sub1 (up to 10), sub2 (up to 20, crossing 10)
- Geometry: geo1 (basic shapes), geo2 (properties)
- Measurement: meas1 (compare sizes), meas2 (clock)

### Adaptive Engine (ZPD)
- Success > 85% → increase difficulty
- Success 70-85% → maintain (optimal zone)
- Success 60-70% → add hints
- Success < 60% → decrease difficulty + scaffolding
- Track response time as secondary indicator
